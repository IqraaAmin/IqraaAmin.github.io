There is a big difference between reading about programming and actually sitting down to write code. You can study syntax, memorize rules, and understand concepts theoretically—but the moment you open a compiler and type your first line of actual code, everything becomes real in a way that no textbook can replicate. My first programming lab session was exactly that kind of moment. It was the point where Computer Engineering stopped being an abstract idea and became something I could touch, test, and experience directly.

Before that day, most of my understanding of programming came from lectures, notes, and discussions with classmates. I knew what variables were, I had heard about loops and conditions, and I understood the basic structure of a program. However, knowing these concepts and actually using them were two completely different experiences. I was excited to finally move beyond theory and see what programming felt like in practice.

## Setting Up for the First Time

Walking into the computer lab for the first time was its own kind of experience. Rows of computers lined the room, each one set up and ready for use. There was a constant hum from the machines, the cool air from the air conditioning, and a focused atmosphere that immediately felt different from a traditional classroom. It was a place built for creating, experimenting, and learning through practice.

Students gradually took their seats while the lab instructor prepared for the session. Some classmates looked confident, while others appeared just as nervous as I was. Everyone knew that this would be one of our first opportunities to apply what we had been learning in lectures.

The instructor began by introducing the development environment we would be using. For many students, including me, it was the first time seeing a professional programming interface. There were menus, toolbars, windows, and options that seemed overwhelming at first glance. The instructor patiently explained the purpose of each section and showed us how to create a new project.

When I finally opened the editor, I was greeted by a blank screen and a blinking cursor. It seemed surprisingly simple after all the setup. Yet that blank screen carried a certain weight because it was waiting for me to tell the computer exactly what to do.

I typed the first line carefully, checking every character before moving on to the next. I had heard countless stories about programming mistakes caused by missing symbols, incorrect capitalization, or misplaced punctuation marks, so I wanted everything to be perfect. Even writing a simple program felt like an important milestone.

As the session continued, students began asking questions and discussing problems with one another. The instructor moved around the room, helping anyone who encountered difficulties. I quickly realized that learning programming was not about getting everything right on the first attempt. It was about experimenting, making mistakes, and gradually understanding how everything worked together.

## The First Error and What It Taught Me

Almost every student in that lab encountered their first error message that day, and I was no exception. After carefully typing what I believed was a correct program, I confidently clicked the button to compile and run it. Instead of the expected output, I was greeted by a wall of red error messages.

For a moment, I felt frustrated. I had spent several minutes checking every line and making sure everything looked correct. Seeing the compiler reject my program was disappointing. My first reaction was to assume something was seriously wrong.

The instructor came over and reviewed my code. Within seconds, they pointed to the issue—a single missing semicolon. One tiny character had prevented the entire program from running.

At first, it seemed almost unbelievable that such a small mistake could create such a big problem. However, that moment taught me one of the most valuable lessons in programming: precision matters. Computers do not make assumptions. They do not interpret your intentions. They follow instructions exactly as they are written.

That experience changed the way I looked at coding. I realized that errors were not signs of failure but opportunities to learn. Every programmer encounters mistakes, regardless of experience level. The difference lies in how they approach those mistakes.

After fixing the semicolon, I compiled the program again. This time, it worked perfectly. The satisfaction I felt was far greater than I expected. Solving even a simple problem gave me confidence and motivated me to keep learning.

As the lab continued, I encountered additional small issues. Sometimes I forgot a bracket, sometimes I typed a command incorrectly, and sometimes I misunderstood the instructions. Each mistake taught me something new. More importantly, I learned how to read error messages and trace problems systematically instead of panicking whenever something went wrong.

Looking back, the errors were actually the most educational part of the session. They forced me to think carefully and understand how programming languages operate. That lesson would remain useful throughout every programming course that followed.

## Growing Comfortable With the Process

By the end of the lab session, I had successfully written and executed my first program. It was a simple program by any standard, but it represented something much larger to me. For the first time, I had created something using code and watched it produce the exact result I intended.

Seeing the output appear on the screen felt surprisingly rewarding. A few lines of text might not seem impressive, but they represented hours of learning, concentration, and effort. At that moment, I finally understood why so many people enjoy programming. There is a unique satisfaction in building something from nothing and watching it come to life through your own instructions.

The experience also changed my perception of Computer Engineering. Until then, programming had seemed like a complex subject filled with difficult concepts and unfamiliar terminology. After the lab, it felt much more approachable. I realized that expertise is built gradually through practice, patience, and persistence.

As I packed my notebook and prepared to leave, I felt both tired and energized. My notebook contained new commands, syntax rules, and observations from the session. My mind was filled with questions about what we would learn next. Most importantly, I left with a growing sense of confidence.

The first programming lab did not make me an expert programmer, nor did it remove all the challenges that lay ahead. What it did provide was proof that I could learn these skills if I remained committed and willing to improve. That proof was incredibly valuable because confidence is often the first step toward mastery.

Looking back now, that first coding session marked the true beginning of my journey as a programmer. It introduced me to the realities of writing code, solving problems, and learning from mistakes. More than anything else, it showed me that programming is not about being perfect—it is about being persistent. And from that day onward, every line of code I wrote became another step forward in that journey.
